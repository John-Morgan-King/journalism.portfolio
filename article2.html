Why Nuclear Power Will Define the Next Australian Election

Date: 16 April 2025

It is one of the great paradoxes of Australian life that in a nation blessed with some of the richest natural energy reserves on Earth — coal, gas, hydro, solar, and uranium — households and businesses still pay some of the highest electricity prices in the developed world. The consequences are not merely economic. Expensive energy is eating away at the middle class, hollowing out manufacturing, and sending investors looking elsewhere. Yet despite this, federal policy seems increasingly committed to phasing out the very resources that made Australia an energy superpower.

With net-zero targets now a political given rather than a debate, the country’s traditional reliance on coal and gas is under siege. Wind and solar are being rolled out at a rapid pace, but they remain plagued by intermittency and an over-reliance on government subsidies. What’s missing, critics say, is a credible plan for baseload power — the kind of consistent, on-demand energy that underpins any serious economy. Enter, once again, nuclear energy.

Once considered political poison, nuclear is undergoing an unlikely renaissance in Canberra. The Coalition, sensing both a policy vacuum and a political opportunity, has made nuclear a central plank of its energy strategy. Peter Dutton’s plan to build small modular reactors (SMRs) on the sites of retiring coal plants has already sparked fierce debate — and, crucially, it’s catching on with voters. Labor, meanwhile, remains firmly opposed, with Climate Change and Energy Minister Chris Bowen dismissing nuclear as too slow, too expensive, and too risky. It’s a stance that risks looking increasingly outdated.

While critics often cite the high upfront costs of nuclear, recent analysis shows that small modular reactors are becoming increasingly competitive — especially when compared to the long-term costs of overbuilding renewable capacity, firming storage, and reinforcing transmission lines to support a scattered energy grid.

Polling tells the story. Support for lifting the ban on nuclear generation — still enshrined in federal law — is now over 50% and climbing. That support is particularly strong among conservative voters, younger Australians, and those in regional communities who have watched jobs disappear as coal plants shut down. There is a growing recognition that if the country is serious about decarbonisation, but unwilling to endure blackouts or unaffordable bills, nuclear needs to be on the table.

Australia’s nuclear awakening is part of a larger global pattern. Countries that once swore off the atom — Japan, South Korea, even Germany — are rethinking their stance. The UK and France are expanding their nuclear fleets, while the United States and Canada are pouring billions into SMRs. The shift is pragmatic, not ideological: a recognition that the green dream must be grounded in reality. Renewables alone cannot do it all. Nuclear, with its low emissions and high output, is being seen not as a rival to wind and solar, but their necessary partner.

This election, then, is shaping up to be more than just a contest between Labor and the Coalition. It’s a referendum on how Australia powers itself into the future — on whether it remains wedded to a costly and unreliable transition or embraces a more balanced approach that includes nuclear as part of the mix. For a country with uranium in the ground and rolling blackouts on the horizon, it’s not just a policy debate. It’s a matter of national self-interest.
